Dear Editors of Molecular Informatics,

We submit for consideration the manuscript "Towards a Formal Definition of
Kinase Inhibitor Selectivity: Empirical Characterization of Definitional
Instability and Proposed Desiderata" as a Research Article.

Kinase inhibitor selectivity is quantified in the literature using at least
four distinct definitions -- the S-score, selectivity entropy, Gini
coefficient, and ratio-based measures -- that are applied interchangeably
without systematic justification. We demonstrate, across two large profiling
datasets (Davis, n=68; Klaeger, n=222), that these definitions are not
interchangeable: they cluster into two families measuring fundamentally
different properties of binding profiles and correlate poorly across families
(r = 0.27-0.48). Counterintuitively, the most definition-stable compounds are
not the most selective but the most promiscuous -- compounds binding nearly the
entire kinome receive consistent rankings across all definitions, while
compounds at intermediate promiscuity receive contradictory rankings depending
on definitional choice.

We identify three mechanistically distinct sources of rank instability, show
that instability is predictable from binding profile shape, and demonstrate
that ratio-based rankings are unreliable at any kinase panel size used in
practice. From these findings we derive four desiderata -- a reliability
threshold condition, bounded gap sensitivity, distributional consistency, and
monotonicity under weak off-target addition -- that any well-formed selectivity
measure should satisfy. No existing definition satisfies all four
simultaneously.

This work is directly relevant to Molecular Informatics as a methodology paper
addressing a fundamental measurement problem in cheminformatics and drug
discovery. We note that the closest prior work, Bosc et al. (2017), was
published in this journal, and our paper extends and formalizes that line of
inquiry. A preprint has been deposited on ChemRxiv
(https://doi.org/10.26434/chemrxiv.15001618/v1).
